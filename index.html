Methodology

How it works

Every morning, before the SDAC auction closes, the pipeline fetches fresh data, retrains, and publishes a new 24-hour forecast with uncertainty bands.

📡
Step 01

Data ingestion

Three years of ENTSO-E hourly data: day-ahead prices, actual load, generation by type (solar, wind, gas, hydro), and the operator's day-ahead RES forecast. Weather from Open-Meteo. UTC-indexed to handle DST correctly.

🔧
Step 02

Feature engineering

Price lags (24/48/168 h), calendar features, day-ahead load forecast, 48-hour generation lags, same-hour RES forecast, and residual load (demand − solar − wind). Every input provably available before 12:00 CET — leakage enforced by tests.

🌲
Step 03

LightGBM model

Gradient-boosted trees with an L1 objective so price spikes don't dominate the fit. The day-ahead RES forecast is the single strongest feature — more expected solar pushes the midday price down (the duck curve).

📊
Step 04

Uncertainty intervals

Split-conformal prediction intervals, calibrated per hour of day on the last 90 days of out-of-sample residuals. Adaptive Conformal Inference (Gibbs & Candès 2021) keeps empirical coverage on target under market drift.

🤖
Step 05

Daily automation

GitHub Actions cron runs every morning: refetch → retrain → forecast → commit. A monthly release-gate re-checks accuracy; ships new calibration only if the model still clearly beats the naive baseline. An afternoon job fills in actual prices for same-day scoring.

Explainability

How the model decides

SHAP attributes each prediction to its input features. The bars show the average impact (EUR/MWh) on the forecast.

Yesterday's price dominates (mean |SHAP| ≈ 19 €/MWh), followed by residual load (≈ 17). More forecast solar/wind lowers residual load and pushes prices down — the duck curve, physically correct.

What each feature means

price_lag_24/48/168h

The price 1, 2 or 7 days ago at the same hour — the past is the strongest clue to the future.

residual_load_fc

Forecast demand minus forecast solar + wind: the load thermal plants must cover — the dominant price driver.

res_fc_solar_mw / wind_mw

The grid operator's own day-ahead forecast of tomorrow's solar/wind output in MW.

load_forecast_mw

The day-ahead forecast of electricity demand.

gen_*_lag_48h

Actual generation by source two days ago (solar, wind, gas, hydro).

wx_*

Weather: solar radiation, wind speed, temperature, cloud cover.

hour / weekday / month / is_holiday

Position in the calendar (Europe/Athens).

Engineering

Key design decisions

🚫

Zero leakage — enforced by tests

Generation lags start at 48 h (not 24 h) because yesterday's afternoon actuals aren't published yet. Tests perturb data at specific cutoffs and assert every earlier feature is unchanged.

🕐

Per-hour calibration

Conformal intervals calibrated separately for each hour. Errors at 04:00 and 20:00 differ by a factor of three — a single global calibration would be badly wrong.

🦆

Duck curve physics

Residual load (demand − solar − wind) is fed explicitly as a feature. A tree can't form this linear combination from separate inputs, but handing the physical quantity directly gives −3.3% MAE.

🛡️

Monthly release gate

A governance module blocks deployment if the challenger doesn't beat the naive baseline by a clear margin, exceed an MAE ceiling, or regresses vs. the last accepted run.

🌍

DST-safe UTC indexing

All data on tz-aware UTC indices. Daylight saving never creates a duplicate or missing hour. 15-minute post-SDAC records averaged to hourly with correct mean aggregation.

🔍

SHAP explainability

TreeExplainer attributes each prediction to its features. The model learned the correct physics — high residual load pushes prices up, sunny midday pushes them down.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

What exactly is this?

An ML system that forecasts tomorrow's 24 hourly wholesale electricity prices for Greece, before the day-ahead auction closes at noon. It runs automatically every morning and publishes the forecast with calibrated uncertainty bands. Educational project — not trading advice.

How accurate is it?

Over a 12-month honest backtest, the mean absolute error is ~16 €/MWh — about 28% better than the standard naive baseline (yesterday's same hour). The Track Record section shows the live, day-by-day performance against real auction prices.

Why do prices sometimes go negative?

On sunny, windy, low-demand hours, solar and wind can produce more than is needed. They have near-zero running cost and must sell, so the price can fall below zero — producers briefly pay to keep feeding the grid. The model captures this through the residual-load feature.

How do I know it isn't peeking at the future?

Every feature is documented and tested to use only information available before the 12:00 CET auction closes. Automated tests perturb future data and assert that earlier forecasts never change. This "no leakage" discipline is the core of honest forecasting.

How often does it update?

Every morning a GitHub Actions job refetches data, retrains, and publishes tomorrow's forecast. An afternoon job fills in the actual prices once the auction publishes, so the track record scores the same day. A monthly job re-checks accuracy behind a quality gate.

Can I use it to trade?

No — it's an educational/portfolio project. Beating a naive baseline doesn't mean beating the market: the auction price already reflects every participant's expectations. Treat the forecast as a demonstration, not financial advice.
